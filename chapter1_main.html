You’ve just now told your parents that you want to live on your own.
They say no b/c it’s not safe and you’re not ready. How do you respond?

OPTION A

React negatively and yell, “What do you mean I’m not ready? I could do this tomorrow!”

OPTION B

I know that you don’t support me on this but it’s what I want so I’m going to begin working on it, on my own

OPTION C

My friend Robert moved out and lives on his own and he used Agency Acme, so I want us to meet with them and see what my options are.

Result

Recognize that you are yelling because you are upset and frustrated. Your parents feel hurt and it takes them three weeks to come around and talk about it again. Instead of yelling, you could say, ““I wish you would respect and support my decision since I am adult. Will you please hear me about because I have spent a lot of time thinking through this.”

You could start this process on your own. Know that this process can be overwhelming, lonely, and frustrating at times. Think about who else is in your life who might have experience with moving who might be able to help you.

Agencies can provide many great resources and supports. Agency Acme might be a great fit for you, but there could be other agencies in your area. Keeping your parents involved is a great way to build in support!

You’ve told your friend Shoshanna that you think you’re ready to live on your own.
Shosh asks you, “How are you going to pay for rent?” You reply:

OPTION A

I’ll start looking around to see if there’s any jobs.

OPTION B

I’ll find a place I like and then figure out the money.

OPTION C

I’ll see what apartment rent costs around me and see if I can do it on my own or if I will need a roommate.

Result

A job can be a great first step to save money before you move out, but know that it can also be a long process.

Remember that many apartments won’t let you sign a lease until you can pay a security deposit and first month’s rent. You might want to think about how you can save up money before searching for a place.

Thinking through what an apartment costs and your personal budget is a great idea before looking for a place to live. Doing research on places near you is a great idea and will help you learn about costs.

You tell your Direct Support Professional that you’d like to live on your own.
They say that you need to keep working on your independent living skills before you are ready to even think about living on your own. What do you do?

OPTION A

You ask your DSP to look at your Individual Service Plan with you so that your goals can be changed.

OPTION B

You yell “I can’t believe you don’t think I’m ready! Why aren’t you supporting me? It’s your job to help me!”

OPTION C

You keep working on the goals already on your ISP with your DSP.

Result

Changing your goals and breaking them down into smaller steps could help you reach your goal of living on your own.

Recognize that you are yelling because you are frustrated and upset. How else could you express that to your DSP? Maybe you could say, “I have thought about this a lot and this is what I really want. Please help me figure out how to help me make it happen.”

By working on your current goals at the pace on your ISP it will take you an extra year or two to gain your DSP’s support to move out on your own.

You tell a sibling that you’re tired of living with your family and ready to live on your own.
They are worried about you being lonely. You reply:

OPTION A

I’ll think about what’s nearby in the community so that I can stay connected with others.

OPTION B

I’ll think about a roommate or a pet.

OPTION C

Won’t you all still be coming to visit me after I move out?

Result

It is okay if you do feel lonely. Everyone feels lonely from time to time, but remember just because you moved out doesn’t mean you can’t make new connections.

This can be a great option to beat loneliness, and a roommate can also help you save money!

It’s okay to be scared of feeling lonely, before you move talk with your family about how often you’ll be able to see each other.

You tell a cousin about your plan to move out on your own and they are so excited to hear this!
You respond by:

OPTION A

You say, “I’m excited, but my mom is trying to control everything!”

OPTION B

You ask, “What was your favorite part about moving out?”

OPTION C

You ask, “What advice do you have for me?”

Result

It’s okay to vent to people because we all need that sometimes. Consider that your cousin is being supportive and you could ask them for advice as well.

Learning what other people enjoyed the most is a great way to stay positive and motivated.

Asking for advice from people who have already moved out is a great way to learn about your new independence.
